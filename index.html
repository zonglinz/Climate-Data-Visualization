About This Visualization

Question and Motivation

The project asks which places are warming the most and which places are warming the fastest. A global average can hide important regional differences, so the visualization starts with a country-level map and then lets viewers drill into the historical record for any selected country.

Data and Transformations

The source data is the CMIP6 CESM2 historical monthly near-surface air temperature variable, tas, from the NOAA Public CMIP6 Dataset on Google Cloud. We used the Amon table, the historical experiment, and the r1i1p1f1 member. The browser does not load the raw Zarr/NetCDF files directly; we preprocessed the data with Python/xarray and saved the result as data/climate_data.json for the D3 webpage.

  1. Loaded the CMIP6 Zarr catalog from Google Cloud and selected CESM2 historical tas.
  2. Loaded CESM2 areacella to get the area of each climate-model grid cell.
  3. Used regionmask Natural Earth country boundaries to assign CMIP6 grid cells to countries.
  4. Resampled monthly temperature values into annual means.
  5. Computed each country's area-weighted annual mean temperature using areacella.
  6. Converted annual temperatures from Kelvin to Celsius.
  7. Used each country's 1850-1900 annual mean as its baseline temperature.
  8. Computed annual anomalies by subtracting that country-specific baseline.
  9. Computed decadal mean anomalies for each country.
  10. Added country-name aliases so the processed country names match the GeoJSON map names.

The map's average-anomaly mode uses the selected years' mean anomaly from the exported annual time series. The warming-rate mode is computed client-side in JavaScript from the same annual anomaly data using a least-squares slope, reported in degrees Celsius per decade. No high-level plotting library is used in the final interactive visualization.

Caveat: this is one CMIP6 model realization, not a direct observational dataset or a multi-model consensus. The visualization is best interpreted as an exploratory model-based view of geographic warming patterns. The final interactive graphic uses D3.js only; Python/xarray were used only to preprocess the raw CMIP6 files into a static JSON file.

Design Rationale

We chose a choropleth world map as the entry point because temperature change is geographic: readers can immediately find familiar places and compare nearby regions. The binned warm color palette makes higher warming visually salient while a gray no-data color avoids implying precision for countries too small to be represented by the model grid.

The map now includes two complementary encodings. Average anomaly answers "how warm is this country relative to its pre-industrial baseline?" Warming rate answers the title question more directly: "how quickly is this country warming during the selected period?" The top-10 ranking panel complements the map because small countries can be difficult to compare spatially, and rankings make the highest values explicit.

We considered a static map, a rotatable globe, and small multiples. A static map would not let the viewer test different time periods. A globe would be visually engaging but would make comparison harder because many countries are hidden at once. Small multiples would show time, but the design would be too crowded for a two-week project. The final design keeps the map as the overview and uses interactive filtering, ranking, tooltips, and country drill-down for exploration.

For visual appeal, we added a zoom animation when a country is clicked, so the map smoothly zooms toward the country before transitioning to its detail view. This gives readers a clear sense of moving “into” a place rather than jumping to an unrelated page. We chose a light, traditional-map aesthetic with a light-blue ocean and soft grid lines because climate is a serious topic and we wanted the data, not the chrome, to feel like the subject.

Interaction Design

The year sliders let users ask questions such as "which countries warmed early?" and "does the ranking change after 1980?" Hover tooltips provide details on demand without cluttering the map. Clicking a country zooms into that country and switches to a detailed view with annual anomalies, decadal averages, and a 30-year rolling warming-rate chart. This interaction supports both overview and close reading: the map shows spatial patterns, while the country view shows temporal dynamics.

Development Process

Time spent. Roughly 25 people-hours total. The breakdown:

  • ~All group members - 4 hours understanding the dataset — The CMIP6 dataset is stored in Zarr on Google Cloud and has non-trivial conventions (Kelvin, cftime calendars, 0–360° longitude, unequal grid-cell areas). So really understanding the structure and how to access the data was challenging in itself.
  • ~All group members - 4 hours on exploratory plots (matplotlib + Plotly) to find a question worth asking. The pattern of individual country-level warming compared to a universal baseline time pointed us at the world-map idea.
  • ~All group members - 6 hours on the country-level aggregation, like area-weighting with areacella, building the regionmask country grid, and resolving the dozens of name mismatches between regionmask's Natural Earth full names and the GeoJSON's short names, such as “United States of America” versus “USA”.
  • ~All group members - 6 hours on the D3 visualization itself: map, color scale, tooltips, drill-down view, sliders, and the zoom animation.
  • ~All group members - 5 hours on styling, the write-up, and final polish.

Most time consuming. Country name matching, by far. After several rounds of console-debugging missing countries, we built an alias dictionary in Python so every country in the visible map gets data. Small islands such as Vatican and Singapore still go uncolored because they are smaller than a single 1°×1° grid cell.
